Publications & research

Secure Blockchain Implementation Using RSA and SHA on FPGA

ECS Transactions (IOP Publishing)

Abstract

Blockchain technology is essential to secure storage, authenticate data, and protect information from misuse. Traditional cryptographic hashing methods (e.g., SHA-0, SHA-1) have limitations including computational overhead, collision risks, scalability constraints, and vulnerability to backtracking. This work proposes an encrypted blockchain block implemented on FPGA using a combined RSA and SHA approach. The design was synthesized and evaluated across Virtex-4, Virtex-5, and Spartan-6 platforms; Virtex-5 was selected based on resource efficiency (slice registers, LUT-FF pairs, memory). Security is strengthened through irreversible hashing and the inability to backtrack data, addressing prior limitations related to security, backtracking, memory usage, and collision risk.

1. Finance Ops & Decision Analytics

Integrated FP&A Decision Suite (Rolling 24-Month Plan)
An FP&A decision-support case simulating budgeting, variance analysis, and rolling 24-month scenario forecasts to surface performance risks and management actions.
GitHub Results

Business context

FP&A teams need a consistent way to monitor actuals vs plan, explain variance drivers, forecast the next 24 months, stress-test downside/inflation scenarios, and translate performance into decision-ready signals. This project uses synthetic corporate finance data created solely for demonstration.

What I built

  • Planning & budget layer: monthly Revenue, COGS, and Opex budgets with assumption tracking (growth, seasonality, inflation).
  • Actuals & variance layer: Budget vs Actual variance ($ and %) with driver-style decomposition (revenue: volume vs price proxy; costs: inflation vs drift proxy).
  • Forecasting & scenarios: rolling 24-month Base, Revenue Downside, and Cost Inflation scenarios with margin/EBITDA impact comparisons.
  • Decision outputs: Green/Amber/Red risk flags for margin compression, expense overruns, and sustained negative variance trends + “what management should do next” summary.

Key insights (example narrative)

  • Revenue variance is concentrated in seasonal periods rather than persistent underperformance.
  • Inflation-driven COGS and fixed Opex drift compress margins under downside scenarios.
  • Under combined downside + inflation, EBITDA margin breaches thresholds → risk escalation + cost-control / target-reset actions.

Assumptions & limitations

Synthetic data (no company data). Driver decomposition is proxy-based to demonstrate methodology. Forecasting uses simplified drivers/seasonality; production would use reconciled GL + operational drivers.

FinTech FP&A: Cost–Margin Sensitivity
Analyzes cost and margin drivers through scenario levers and sensitivity modeling to quantify downside risk and support operating and planning decisions.
GitHub Results

Business context

In FinTech FP&A, leadership needs fast, driver-based visibility into what is moving margin (take-rate, loss/chargebacks, processing costs, and operating expense) and how sensitive profitability is to small changes in key levers. This project uses synthetic data for demonstration.

What I built

  • Unit economics model: revenue and cost structure tied to core margin drivers (e.g., take-rate, losses, cost per transaction, fixed opex).
  • Sensitivity framework: one- and two-way sensitivity analysis across levers (take-rate, loss rate, cost inflation) to quantify EBITDA/margin impact.
  • Decision visuals: sensitivity heatmaps and scenario summaries to highlight breakpoints where margin compression becomes material.

Decision use-cases

  • Stress-test downside: loss/chargeback deterioration, take-rate compression, or cost inflation.
  • Identify the highest-impact levers for operating actions (pricing, risk controls, cost optimization).
  • Communicate “what changes matter” using executive-friendly heatmaps.

Assumptions & limitations

Synthetic demo data. Levers are simplified to illustrate methodology; production would connect to reconciled GL + operational drivers and add governance/versioning for assumptions.

2. Customer & Merchant Risk Analytics

Customer Risk Segmentation (Risk Tiering Framework)
A risk segmentation case that tiers customers into actionable cohorts to prioritize monitoring intensity, reviews, and interventions.

Business context

Risk teams need a scalable way to identify higher-risk customers early, prioritize monitoring and investigative capacity, and apply differentiated controls without increasing friction for low-risk cohorts. This case study demonstrates a risk-based segmentation approach using synthetic signals for portfolio-style prioritization.

Objective

Segment customers into interpretable risk cohorts and map them into Low/Medium/High tiers that drive monitoring cadence, escalation thresholds, and targeted interventions.

What I built

  • Signal engineering: customer-level indicators (velocity, concentration, behavioral volatility, historical-risk proxies) aggregated at cohort level.
  • Segmentation & tiering: cohort clustering / scoring logic mapped into clear risk tiers with stability and interpretability checks.
  • Risk-based actions: tier-linked monitoring intensity, review frequency, and escalation readiness (who/what/why evidence).

Decision outputs

  • Customer-to-tier assignment and tier distribution across the portfolio.
  • High-risk cohort identification to prioritize enhanced monitoring and interventions.
  • Operational playbook alignment: “monitor more” vs “review periodically” vs “standard” by tier.

Assumptions & limitations

Demonstration only (synthetic signals; no company data). Indicators are proxies to show methodology; production implementations would validate tiers against investigation outcomes, losses, and policy thresholds.

Transaction & Account Risk Monitoring Framework (Outlier Detection & Escalation)
A monitoring case study that uses rule-based signals to detect anomalous transactions and risky account patterns, aligned to investigation workflows and escalation-ready documentation.

Business context

Transaction and account monitoring programs need consistent, explainable logic to surface outliers, reduce noise, and route risk to the right investigative path. This case study demonstrates a rule-based monitoring framework that connects signals → alerts → cases → escalation with clear evidence and audit-ready traceability.

Objective

Detect suspicious patterns early (velocity, concentration, threshold breaches, and behavioral shifts), prioritize review queues, and standardize investigative and escalation steps to support timely decisions.

What I built

  • Signal library: threshold and pattern rules for transaction outliers and account-level anomalies (velocity, spikes, unusual geography/device, repetition, and concentration proxies).
  • Alert logic: severity scoring / bucketing to reduce false positives and focus on decision-relevant signals.
  • Case workflow alignment: investigation checklist and evidence fields mapped to escalation criteria (what happened, why it triggered, and what action was taken).
  • Governance-ready outputs: documented decisions, risk rationale, and audit trail for reviews and policy alignment.

Decision outputs

  • Alert queue with severity tiers and recommended next steps (review, monitor, escalate).
  • Consistent case narratives and evidence capture to support escalations and stakeholder reviews.
  • Monitoring coverage map: which risks are addressed by rules vs where gaps remain.

Assumptions & limitations

Demonstration-only framework. Rules illustrate methodology and explainability; production monitoring would tune thresholds using historical outcomes, incorporate entity resolution and richer device/network signals, and track precision/recall and investigator workload.
